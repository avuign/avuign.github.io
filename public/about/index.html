About

Background#

I’m a final year PhD student in theoretical physics at EPFL. I work in the Fields and Strings laboratory where I conduct research in quantum field theory and quantum gravity. Before that, I did my Bachelor’s and Master’s at EPFL.

What drew me to physics is the idea that complex phenomena can be reduced to simple, universal equations. A few principles explain an enormous range of observations. Physics is fundamentally about finding what’s essential and discarding what isn’t, until you’re left with the minimal structure that actually matters.

This is also what I look for in problems: situations where careful thinking can cut through apparent complexity and reveal something simple underneath.

Research#

My PhD explores two related questions: what are the fundamental constraints on quantum field theories, and how does spacetime emerge from more primitive ingredients?

S-matrix bootstrap. One line of work uses the S-matrix bootstrap, a method that extracts physical predictions from consistency conditions like unitarity and crossing symmetry, without assuming a specific Lagrangian. With collaborators, I developed tools to incorporate UV data (like the central charge of a CFT) into IR observables, and applied this to Ising Field Theory and the O(N) nonlinear sigma model. This involves formulating physics problems as semidefinite programs and solving them numerically on clusters.

Matrix models and emergent gravity. The other line asks how classical spacetime can arise from a theory with no geometry to begin with. I work on matrix models, particularly the IKKT and BMN models, which are conjectured to be non-perturbative definitions of string/M-theory. Using supersymmetric localization and holography, we’ve shown explicit matchings between matrix model observables and gravitational quantities, including constructing the dual geometries and computing partition functions exactly.

Transition to Machine Learning#

I have recently deciding to stop my career in high energy theoretical physics to look for a job in applied research in machine learning.

A few reasons, in order of honesty:

It’s intellectually challenging. The field is young, the theory lags behind practice, and there are genuinely open questions about why things work. That’s the kind of environment I like.

The skillset transfers. My background trained me to formalize vague questions, find the right mathematical structure, and write code to get answers. ML research seems to need exactly this. One has to understand what models actually learn, why certain architectures work, and how to make training more principled.

It matters now. Theoretical physics operates on long timescales. Some questions I’ve worked on were asked 50 years ago and might take another 50 to resolve. I want to work on problems where I can see the impact within my lifetime, and where the answers actually change something.

Beyond work#

When I’m not at a whiteboard or a screen, I’m usually hiking. Living in Switzerland makes that easy. I like being in contact with nature — it’s a good counterbalance to abstract work.
